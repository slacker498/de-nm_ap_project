Simulation Results & Plot Analysis

Comparing circuit behavior under different resistor values (damping coefficients) and input voltage types.

1. System Response Under Damping Cases

As stated, RLC Series circuits can be analyzed with respect to their behavior under different damping conditions. A MATLAB simulation of such behaviors was performed using the initial code implementation of the base second-order differential equation. Conditions analyzed included underdamping with an AC voltage source, as well as underdamping, critical damping, and overdamping, all with a DC voltage source.

Case 1 • Underdamped AC

AC Voltage Source (Underdamped)

Resistor: \(R = 2\ \Omega\) (Underdamped)
Voltage Source: \(V(t) = 100 \sin(60t)\text{ V}\)
Inductance & Capacitance: \(L = 1\text{ H}\), \(C = 0.01\text{ F}\)

Due to this low resistance and the continuously changing AC source voltage, the charge and rate of change of charge (current) oscillate heavily. The circuit oscillates for a very long time without settling, which cannot be plotted in full duration due to its large value. The transient response takes a substantial period to decay, leaving the steady-state sinusoidal response.

Figure b: Plot of q(t) changing with t for an AC source V = 100sin(60t) V, R = 2 ohms (Underdamped)
Case 2 • Underdamped DC

DC Voltage Source (Underdamped)

Resistor: \(R = 2\ \Omega\) (Underdamped)
Voltage Source: \(V = 100\text{ V}\) (Constant DC)
Inductance & Capacitance: \(L = 1\text{ H}\), \(C = 0.01\text{ F}\)

Similar to the AC source, the system exhibits large initial oscillations. However, because the voltage source is constant (DC), the transient term eventually decays to zero, and the system gradually settles to its steady-state charge. The steady-state charge stored on the capacitor is \(q = C \cdot V = 0.01 \cdot 100 = 1\text{ C}\).

Figure c: Plot of q(t) changing with t for a DC source V = 100 V, R = 2 ohms (Underdamped)
Case 3 • Critically Damped DC

DC Voltage Source (Critically Damped)

Resistor: \(R = 20\ \Omega\) (Critically Damped, \(\alpha = \omega_0\))
Voltage Source: \(V = 100\text{ V}\) (Constant DC)
Inductance & Capacitance: \(L = 1\text{ H}\), \(C = 0.01\text{ F}\)

When the resistance is set to \(20\ \Omega\), the system is critically damped. The circuit reaches its steady state of \(1\text{ C}\) in the shortest possible time without any oscillation. This response makes it desired for systems requiring fast and stable responses.

Figure d: Plot of q(t) changing with t for a DC source V = 100 V, R = 20 ohms (Critically damped)
Case 4 • Overdamped DC

DC Voltage Source (Overdamped)

Resistor: \(R = 100\ \Omega\) (Overdamped, \(\alpha > \omega_0\))
Voltage Source: \(V = 100\text{ V}\) (Constant DC)
Inductance & Capacitance: \(L = 1\text{ H}\), \(C = 0.01\text{ F}\)

With \(R = 100\ \Omega\), the resistor provides excessive damping. There are no oscillations, but the circuit returns to equilibrium very slowly compared to the critically damped case. Although sluggish, this behavior is useful in surge protection circuits where suppressing rapid changes is more critical than response speed.

Figure e: Plot of q(t) changing with t for a DC source V = 100 V, R = 100 ohms (Overdamped)
