Mathematical Modeling & Derivation

Applying physical laws and calculus to characterize the exchange of energy in an RLC Series Circuit.

1. Circuit Description & Problem Definition

To really understand why electrical devices don’t just jump to action when they are plugged in or turned on, we need to understand what RLC (Resistor, Inductor, Capacitor) circuits are and the mathematics behind them. When we apply KVL (Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law) to a standard series RLC circuit, we uncover a second-order differential equation that forms the basis of all of our analysis in this project.

Figure a: Standard RLC Series Circuit Diagram (Resistor R, Inductor L, Capacitor C in series with Voltage Source V(t))

Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law states that the algebraic sum of all voltages around any closed loop in a circuit must equal zero. Applying KVL to our series loop, the sum of the voltage drops across the resistor, inductor, and capacitor must equal the applied voltage source \(F(t)\):

Governing ODE
$$L\frac{d^2q}{dt^2} + R\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}q(t) = F(t)$$

Variable Definitions:

F(t)
Voltage Source The external forcing function applied as a function of time (V).
q(t)
Electric Charge The instantaneous charge accumulated on the capacitor plates (C).
L
Inductance The measure of the inductor's opposition to changes in current flow (H).
R
Resistance The measure of energy dissipation in the system (ohms, Ω).
C
Capacitance The capacity of the capacitor to store electrical charge (F).
i(t)
Current \(i(t) = \frac{dq}{dt}\) The flow of electric charge, which is the rate of change of charge (A).

Model Assumptions:

  • The resistor only provides linear resistance \(R\), governed by Ohm's Law (\(V_R = R i\)).
  • The inductor only provides linear inductance \(L\), governed by Faraday's Law (\(V_L = L \frac{di}{dt}\)).
  • The capacitor only stores charge with constant capacitance \(C\), governed by (\(V_C = \frac{q}{C}\)).
  • At every instant, the voltage around a closed loop is 0 (KVL applies perfectly).
  • The parameter values \(R\), \(L\), and \(C\) are lumped and constant (they do not vary with time or current level).

2. Damping Dynamics

As George Maria Selvam and Vignesh (2018) point out, we can simplify our analysis of the circuit by looking at the interaction of two rival physical characteristics: the damping coefficient (\(\alpha\)) and the natural resonant frequency (\(\omega_0\)).

System Friction

Damping Coefficient (\(\alpha\))

Determined entirely by the resistor, which actively works to slow the current down and dissipate energy as heat.

$$\alpha = \frac{R}{2L}$$
System Vibrancy

Natural Resonant Frequency (\(\omega_0\))

Represents the circuit’s natural desire to oscillate, dictated by the rate at which the inductor and capacitor swap stored energy.

$$\omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$$

The relationship between \(\alpha\) and \(\omega_0\) determines which of the three response modes the circuit will exhibit when disturbed:

Damping State Mathematical Condition Physical Behavior Applications
Underdamped \(\alpha < \omega_0\) (Low R) Current and charge swing back and forth (oscillate) with decaying amplitude. Radio tuning, filters, transmitters.
Critically Damped \(\alpha = \omega_0\) (Optimal R) Returns to equilibrium in the minimum possible time without any oscillations. Measuring instruments, CD players.
Overdamped \(\alpha > \omega_0\) (High R) Slowly decays back to equilibrium without any oscillations. Surge suppressors, protection circuits.

3. Analytical Solution Attempt

The initial attempt to solve the differential equation was performed under the assumption that the voltage source is a direct current source (DC), with initial conditions of \(q(0) = 0\) and \(q'(0) = 0\) (charge and current are zero when the circuit closes). This analytical solution focuses on the critically damped and overdamped states.

Initial Parameter Setup (from project configurations):

  • Inductance \(L = 1 \text{ H}\)
  • Capacitance \(C = 0.01 \text{ F}\) (Natural resonant frequency \(\omega_0 = 10 \text{ rad/s}\))
  • Applied Voltage \(V_{DC} = 100 \text{ V}\)

Derivation Breakdown & Equations

Below is the exact step-by-step mathematical derivation of the analytical solutions based on the KVL equation.

1. Characteristic Equation & Roots

From the governing ODE: $$L\frac{d^2q}{dt^2} + R\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}q(t) = F(t)$$

The homogeneous equation is: $$L r^2 + R r + \frac{1}{C} = 0$$

Substituting the fixed parameters \(L = 1\), \(C = 0.01\): $$r^2 + R r + 100 = 0$$

Using the quadratic formula, the roots are: $$r_{1,2} = \frac{-R \pm \sqrt{R^2 - 400}}{2}$$

2. Overdamped Solution (\(R = 100\ \Omega\))

For \(R = 100\), the roots are real and distinct: $$r_{1,2} = \frac{-100 \pm \sqrt{100^2 - 400}}{2} = -50 \pm 48.99$$

$$r_1 = -1.01, \quad r_2 = -98.99$$

The general solution is: $$q(t) = c_1 e^{-1.01t} + c_2 e^{-98.99t} + q_p$$

With \(F(t) = 100\), the particular solution is \(q_p = C \times 100 = 0.01 \times 100 = 1\).

Applying initial conditions \(q(0) = 0\) and \(q'(0) = 0\):

$$0 = c_1 + c_2 + 1 \implies c_2 = -1 - c_1$$

$$0 = -1.01 c_1 - 98.99 c_2 \implies -1.01 c_1 - 98.99(-1 - c_1) = 0 \implies 97.98 c_1 = -98.99 \implies c_1 = -1.01$$

$$c_2 = 0.01$$

Final Equation: $$q(t) = 1 - 1.01 e^{-1.01 t} + 0.01 e^{-98.99 t}$$

3. Critically Damped Solution (\(R = 20\ \Omega\))

For \(R = 20\), the discriminant is zero, yielding a repeated root: $$r = \frac{-20 \pm \sqrt{400 - 400}}{2} = -10$$

The general solution is: $$q(t) = (c_1 + c_2 t) e^{-10t} + 1$$

Applying \(q(0) = 0\): $$0 = c_1 + 1 \implies c_1 = -1$$

Applying \(q'(0) = 0\): $$q'(t) = c_2 e^{-10t} - 10(c_1 + c_2 t) e^{-10t}$$

$$0 = c_2 - 10(-1) \implies c_2 = -10$$

Final Equation: $$q(t) = 1 - (1 + 10 t) e^{-10 t}$$

4. Underdamped Solution (\(R = 2\ \Omega\))

For \(R = 2\), the roots are complex conjugates: $$r_{1,2} = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 400}}{2} = -1 \pm i\sqrt{99}$$

The general solution is: $$q(t) = e^{-t} (c_1 \cos(\sqrt{99} t) + c_2 \sin(\sqrt{99} t)) + 1$$

Applying \(q(0) = 0\): $$0 = c_1 + 1 \implies c_1 = -1$$

Applying \(q'(0) = 0\): $$0 = -c_1 + \sqrt{99} c_2 \implies 1 + \sqrt{99} c_2 = 0 \implies c_2 = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{99}}$$

Final Equation: $$q(t) = 1 - e^{-t} \left(\cos(\sqrt{99} t) + \frac{1}{\sqrt{99}} \sin(\sqrt{99} t)\right)$$
